Takeaways from AR/VR VAP Session

Understanding AR & VR

Augmented Reality (AR) overlays digital elements (text, images, 3D objects) onto the real world. It enhances real-world environments by integrating virtual components, visible through devices like smartphones, tablets, and AR headsets.

Virtual Reality (VR) creates a completely immersive digital experience where users can interact with a fully simulated environment using VR headsets like the Meta Quest.

Types of AR

Marker-Based AR

Marker-based AR requires a predefined visual marker, such as a QR code or a printed image, to trigger digital content. The camera scans the marker and overlays virtual objects onto it.

  • Used in educational tools, books, and toys to enhance learning experiences.
  • Examples: Wall decals, toys, tattoos, encyclopedia books, action flashcards (e.g., HoloKitab from Shark Tank India), storybooks, and mobile skins.

Markerless AR

Markerless AR does not require predefined markers. Instead, it uses technologies like SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping) to understand the environment and place virtual objects dynamically.

  • Used in applications that require real-time interaction with surroundings.
  • Examples: Pokémon GO, real-life space overlays, and superimposed holograms.

Ways to Experience AR

  • Web-Based AR: AR experiences accessible directly via web browsers without requiring app installations.
  • Mobile Apps: AR applications developed for Android and iOS, such as the IKEA Place app, which allows users to visualize furniture in their home.
  • Social Media AR: Platforms enabling users to create and use AR effects, such as:
    • SPARK AR (Meta): Used for creating AR filters on Instagram and Facebook.
    • SNAP AR (Lens Studio 5.6.0): Snapchat’s AR creation tool for interactive effects.
    • Effect House (TikTok): Enables TikTok users to build custom AR experiences.

IoT and AR Integration

AR is being used to visualize IoT-enabled home appliances before purchasing or installation. Users can see how smart home devices like security systems, lighting, and thermostats will look and function in their space.

Hands-On Activities

A-Frame

A-Frame is an open-source web framework built on Three.js, designed to make AR and VR development accessible through HTML-like syntax. It is widely used for creating immersive experiences without requiring extensive programming knowledge.

Why A-Frame?

  • Allows users to create AR/VR scenes using simple HTML elements.
  • Supports WebXR, making it compatible with both VR headsets and AR-enabled devices.
  • Can be used with AR.js for developing marker-based and markerless AR applications.

What We Did: We used A-Frame to create basic AR environments and experimented with marker-based tracking.

Snap AR (Lens Studio)

Snap AR, powered by Lens Studio, is a powerful tool used to create AR filters (Lenses) for Snapchat. It enables creators to design interactive experiences with face tracking, body tracking, and world AR effects.

Why Lens Studio?

  • Enables developers and designers to create interactive filters and effects.
  • Supports AI-powered facial recognition and 3D object placement.
  • Provides real-time preview and debugging for better development workflow.

What We Did: We created a custom Leopard Face Filter using Lens Studio’s face tracking features.

Other Activities

  • Downloaded and explored Blender for 3D modeling.
  • Created a Sketchfab account to browse and share 3D models.

VR Technologies

VR immerses users in fully digital environments. The session covered different VR platforms and devices.

  • Meta Quest 2 & 3: Standalone VR headsets by Meta, providing wireless VR experiences.
  • Meta Horizon: A VR social platform where users can interact in a virtual space.
  • SideQuest: A third-party tool that allows installing and streaming custom VR apps on Meta Quest.
